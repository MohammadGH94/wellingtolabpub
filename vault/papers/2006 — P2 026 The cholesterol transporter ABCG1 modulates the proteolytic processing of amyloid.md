## Abstract
> Reconstructed from OpenAlex inverted index — word order is correct, punctuation may be approximate.

Extracellular deposits of aggregated amyloid beta peptides (Aβ) derived from the proteolytic processing of amyloid precursor protein (APP) are a neuropathological hallmark of Alzheimer's Disease (AD). The cleavage of APP by secretases is modulated by the level and distribution of intracellular cholesterol. However, the influence of genes involved in lipid trafficking on the processing of APP remains poorly understood. The cholesterol transporter ABCG1 regulates the distribution and efflux of cholesterol and is highly expressed in the brain. Notably, the human ABCG1 gene maps to chromosome 21q22.3 and is inherited in excess in Down Syndrome (DS). Because individuals with DS typically manifest with AD neuropathology in their mid to late 30s, inheritance of excess ABCG1 may contribute to the accelerated onset of AD in DS. Here we show that expression of ABCG1 in HEK–APPswe cells results in increased cholesterol efflux and an accelerated production of Aβ 40 and Aβ 42. Mutation of the conserved ABC signature sequence in ABCG1 blocks cholesterol efflux and reduces Aβ secretion to baseline levels. Western blotting and biotinylation experiments reveal that ABCG1–expressing cells have increased total and cell–surface associated APP, as well as an increase in sAPPα secretion, suggesting that elevated substrate availability may underlie the elevated APP processing in the presence of ABCG1. Finally, we show that ABCG1 is overexpressed by 2–fold in DS frontal cortex compared to control and AD brain (p<0.001). DS cortex also exhibits increased Aβ levels and elevated sAPP/total APP compared to age–matched controls. Our results suggest that inheritance of excess ABCG1 in DS brain may contribute to the very early onset of AD in DS subjects.
