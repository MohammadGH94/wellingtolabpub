## Abstract
> Reconstructed from OpenAlex inverted index — word order is correct, punctuation may be approximate.

Increasing evidence suggests that altered cholesterol metabolism may play a role in the pathogenesis of Alzheimer's Disease (AD). Recently, stimulation of liver-X-receptor (LXR)-responsive genes with synthetic LXR agonists has been shown to improve both cognitive and neuropathological phenotypes in AD mouse models. The cholesterol transporters ABCA1 and ABCG1 are LXR-responsive genes that are expressed in brain. The objective of this study was to define the individual contributions of ABCA1 and ABCG1 to brain lipid metabolism and AD pathogenesis. Mice with genetic alterations of ABCA1 and ABCG1 levels were crossed with PDAPP mice, followed by analysis of apoE, Aβ, and amyloid levels. Elimination of ABCA1 reduces apoE levels and lipidation in brain and promotes amyloidogenesis in PDAPP mice and other animal models of AD. Conversely, selective overexpression of ABCA1 in brain is sufficient to increase apoE lipidation and suppress the formation of amyloid plaques in PDAPP mice. In contrast, selective deletion or overexpression of ABCG1 does not affect Aβ, amyloid, or apoE levels, or cognitive performance of PDAPP mice. The beneficial effects of LXR agonists for AD may be due to changes in ABCA1 and apoE rather than ABCG1.
