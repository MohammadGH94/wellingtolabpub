## Abstract
> Reconstructed from OpenAlex inverted index — word order is correct, punctuation may be approximate.

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a major threat to the autonomy, mobility, and well-being of persons in the developed world. The etiology of this disease is not yet clearly defined; however, recent work has implicated oligomers of the Aβ peptide in neurotoxicity, inhibition of synaptic plasticity, and cell-to-cell spread of AD pathology. The atomic level structure of Aβoligomers has not been conclusively solved, but biochemical, biophysical and immunochemical data suggest a fibril/oligomer model in which Aβ monomers are packed in broad b-hairpin steric zipper structures. As insoluble Aβ fibril structures possess an internal K28-D23 salt bridge, we hypothesized that soluble oligomers would favor externally oriented charged side chains. Inspection of Aβ structural models revealed solvent exposed epitopes at the Aβ N-terminus recognized by the monoclonal antibodies 6E10 and 4G8, and a constrained turn comprised of residues 25-29 (GSNKG), against which no antibody has been reported. We raised antibodies against this epitope (Fig. 1a) in mice and evaluated them with Biacore surface plasmon resonance, immunoblotting after SDS-PAGE and Blue Native-PAGE, and flow cytometry. A monoclonal antibody (5E3) raised against the cyclic GSNKG epitope binds to synthetic oligomers of Aβ with high affinity (∼7 nM), but is unreactive to monomeric Aβ or full-length amyloid precursor protein. 5E3 specifically recognizes oligomers in Alzheimer's diseased brain homogenates (Fig. 1b & c) and cerebrospinal fluid, while displaying negligible reactivity to healthy control brain and cerebrospinal fluid. 5E3 is detecting a SDS-resistant, HFIP and Proteinase K sensitive ∼56 kDa oligomer in AD brains. Analysis of 5E3 reactivity to normal mouse neurons confirmed that the novel epitope is absent from neuronal cell surfaces, unlike previously known epitopes and reactive antibodies (i.e. 6E10). Furthermore, 5E3 treatment prevented Aβ (1-40) oligomer-induced neurotoxicity in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 1d) and stalled Aβ oligomerization in vitro.
