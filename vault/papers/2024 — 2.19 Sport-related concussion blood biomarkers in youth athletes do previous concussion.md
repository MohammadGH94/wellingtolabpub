## Abstract
> Reconstructed from OpenAlex inverted index — word order is correct, punctuation may be approximate.

<h3>Objective</h3> To characterize levels of plasma GFAP, NF-L, UCH-L1, T-tau, and p-tau181 in healthy youth athletes considering the influence of previous concussion, age, sex, menstrual cycle stage, and sex hormone concentrations. <h3>Design</h3> Cohort study. <h3>Setting</h3> Canadian high-school and community sport settings. <h3>Participants</h3> Healthy youth athletes participating in the SHRed Concussions study (n=149, 48% female, ages 11–17). <h3>Assessment of Risk Factors</h3> Previous concussion (Yes/No), age (years), sex (M/F), menstrual cycle stage (follicular/luteal), plasma estradiol, progesterone, and testosterone. <h3>Outcome Measures</h3> Natural log (ln) transformed plasma GFAP, NF-L, UCH-L1, T-tau, and p-tau181 concentrations analyzed using multi-level multivariable linear regression (α=0.05). <h3>Main Results</h3> Males displayed higher ln-NF-L (β=0.194; 95%CI: 0.014, 0.375; p=0.035) and ln-p-tau181 (β=0.236; 95%CI: 0.081, 0.390; p=0.003), but lower ln-UCH-L1 (β=-0.166; 95%CI: -0.298, -0.034; p=0.013) and ln-GFAP (β=-0.168; 95%CI: -0.304, -0.032; p=0.016) concentrations than females. ln-GFAP was negatively associated with age (β=-0.064; 95%CI: -0.107, -0.021; p=0.004). Progesterone was positively associated with ln-p-tau181 (β=0.009; 95%CI: 0.0004, 0.0173; p=0.049) in females. Estradiol (β=0.005; 95%CI: 0.001, 0.008; p=0.014) and testosterone (β=0.031; 95%CI: 0.011, 0.052; p=0.003) were positively associated with ln-NF-L in males. Previous concussion and menstrual cycle stage were not associated with any biomarker concentrations. <h3>Conclusions</h3> Age and sex-specific reference intervals may be warranted for pediatric athlete populations prior to clinical validation of blood biomarkers for sport-related concussion. Hormonal associations indicate the need to consider puberty and development in adolescent studies. Data suggests these biomarkers are resilient to a history of previous concussion and menstrual cycle stage, supporting their continued research in youth sport-related concussion.
